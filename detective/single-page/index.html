Post-Boom Detective Fiction and Neo-Noir: Perder es cuestión de método La sangre y la lluvia
Hello again everyone, and welcome to this lecture on Post-Boom Detective Fiction and Neo-Noir. The text and films I'll be looking at today correspond to wide and influential cultural interest in this genre in the post-Boom era in Latin America. It's a genre which seems to speak urgently to the contemporary political, social and economic landscape of many Latin American countries, from Brazil and Argentina to Colombia, Cuba and Mexico. Even if you're not studying the specific texts I'll be discussing today, you should find the conceptual framework useful for understanding the shifts at work between the classic detective figure as purveyor of reason, and the contemporary corrupted or 'fallen' investigator, who is more often than not a journalist or an ordinary citizen who finds themselves embroiled against their will in a drama of crime and corruption, or worse, is complicit with it. We'll trace the origin of the postmodern detective in the stories of Borges, and we'll be discussing why the neo-noir genre in literature and film seems to have such salience in the region. We’ll also be looking at the relationship of neo-noir to biopolitics and necropolitics (terms which I shall explain).
But before we launch in, I just have to add a brief CONTENT WARNING: in this lecture there will be mentions of and in some instances images and film clips representing the following: nudity, sexual activity, consumption and injection of narcotics, and violence including images of wounds and blood, and dead bodies. The film La sangre y la lluvia also contains a representation of attempted rape and murder (not shown in this lecture). These mentions or images are not gratuitious and will be dealt with sensitively as part of an intellectual discussion of why the genres of detective fiction and neo-noir seem so suited as interpretations of some of the problems besetting contemporary Latin American societies.

2. Crime fiction, noir, and "why Colombia"?
As mentioned in my introduction, the genres of detective fiction and neo-noir seem particularly well suited for interpreting some of the problems that beset contemporary Latin American societies. I'll divide the explanation of why this should be so into two, one obvious, and the other less obvious but equally important. But first of all I should just explain what neo-noir is.
2.1 Noir and neo-noir
The noir genre is one most closely associated with film, though it has crossed back over into fiction as well. Noir as a film genre refers to the films of the 1940s and 50s in the USA that deal with crime, prohibition of substances, the urban underworld and police corruption in the aftermath of alcohol prohibition in the US and the rise of gangsterism associated with Prohibition. A particular feature of noir is its mobilization of post-War anxieties surrounding female sexuality as an image for the social decay and corruption besetting American cities. Women had been enticed out to work in munitions factories and in numerous traditionally masculine jobs during the Second World War when so many young men were away fighting, and after the War, they did not so easily want to return to a life of domesticity and subordination to men. Women were asserting their independence in society, in workplaces and also sexually, and Hollywood, at least in its B-movies, captured these anxieties by mapping them onto the femme-fatale figures that populate the noir films of the 40s and 50s.
2.1.1 The femme fatale
The femme fatale is a female protagonist in noir films who somehow represents the libidinal disorder of this world of illicit substances, prohibition and corruption. Many of the anxieties concerning the breakdown of conventional family structures and patriarchal social order are projected onto the femme fatale, and she is often subjected to punishment for her wayward sexuality, or else if she does not die, she is ultimately quelled by marriage and domesticity when order and authority are restored at the end of some of the films.
2.1.2 Neo-noir
The best noir films have more ambiguous endings where nothing is quite resolved, and corruption amongst the police -- who are often in the pay of the gangsters -- is never rooted out fully. This is even more the case of neo-noir, a revival genre that makes its appearance in the 1970s and 80s, but that refers to the even greater anxieties now caused by our immersion in a global system of unaccountable power regulated by superpowers and multinational corporations. A great example is Roman Polanski's Chinatown of 1974. In classic noir, for example, The Big Sleep of 1946, based on the novel by Raymond Chandler, the local or municipal order, which has been disrupted by the social, economic and libidinal corruption, is restored at the end of the film along with the final taming or internment of the femme fatale, with the help of some honest policemen and magistrates who act as agents of a moral order. However, in Chinatown, the conspiracies proliferate and extend outwards indefinitely, so that we suspect them to be synonymous with the crisis-ridden, expanding envelope of the global capitalist system itself. Just before she is deliberately shot through her head and eye by a policeman at the end of the movie while attempting to escape over the border to Mexico, the fleeing Evelyn screams at the detective who is always one step behind unfolding events, that there is no point letting the police handle the situation, because her corrupt millionaire father ‘owns the police’.

Neo-noir: Chinatown (Roman Polanski,1973)
2.2 Crime, corruption, violence
Of course Latin American societies are no strangers to crime, corruption and violence, and the rapid urbanization of many countries since the 1950s means that insecurity on the streets of the cities and megalopolises, together with an ineffective police force, is rife. Therefore this is an obvious reason why detective fiction and crime fiction, including noir and neo-noir, seem to be particularly apt ways of providing a symbolic translation of so many of the concerns of contemporary citizens in countries like Argentina, Brazil, Colombia and Mexico. While Mexican popular cinema of the 1940s and 50s did dabble in their own version of noir, often importing the figure of the "bad girl" whose wayward sexuality was disruptive to the moral patriarchal order of society, for the most part this is translated into concerns over the incompatibilities of traditional Catholic icons of womanly virtue and modern urban life. Neo-noir has had a much greater impact on contemporary fiction and film, perhaps reflecting and expressing the extent to which contemporary Latin American inhabitants of mega-cities are unable to comprehend the vast scale of global systems of power and control over which they have little say or influence. The high levels of impunity and official corruption add to this erosion of moral trust.

This is the context in which the contemporary detective figures or their proxies must operate, one in which it is not so easy to separate the guilty form the innocent.
2.3 The "meaning" / senselessness of national trauma
That is perhaps the "obvious" interpretation of why detective and crime fiction, and in particular neo-noir, seems to be so apt for contemporary Latin America. The less obvious reason has to do with the detective figure as someone who is traditionally charged with restoring meaning and reason to a disordered landscape of crime, to discover the perpetrator, solve the riddle and, above all, restore an ordered and comprehensible narrative to the shattered narrative represented by the scattered clues of the crime being investigated. In countries which have been through senseless national traumas, which is to say traumas which shake the very narrative of the nation and which seem to defy rational narrative and explanation, the contemporary detective figure seems to offer a faint hope of restoring narrative and meaning, perhaps restoring some order. As we'll see, this hope is more often than not confounded, but it doesn't stop the investigators of these novels and films from trying to piece the truth together, to restore meaning to an irrevocably shattered text.
2.4 Why Colombia?
As for why Colombia particularly, let's ask one of the filmmakers most associated with this genre in Colombia for his reasons for using a parody of the noir genre in his landmark film Soplo de vida of 1999, a film that perhaps marks the beginning of the neo-noir movement in Colombia. Although I won't have time to examine it today in any detail, it is an important filmic antecedent. Here he is, in an article he wrote about the making of his film:
2.4.1 No estoy de acuerdo con los que creen que el cine negro es algo ajeno a nosotros. Siempre he pensado que es lo más propio. […]. Uno no necesita ninguna autorización para hacer cine negro. El film justifica los medios. Crimen organizado. Policía corrupta. Caos político. Prohibición de sustancias. Ajustes de cuentas. Terrorismo. Masacres. Paranoia. Impunidad total. Todos los colombianos conocemos esa historia. Vivimos todos los días una película de cine negro. Así como en Estados Unidos existió la Prohibición, en Colombia tuvimos la tolerancia a las drogas. […] Después de más de 25 años de cultivos ilegales, ya tenemos la suficiente madurez para recoger los frutos podridos de esta cosecha roja. El cine negro norteamericano nació en un momento de caos y pesimismo generalizado, producto de la paranoia anticomunista […]. Creo que esto explica por qué nosotros optamos por retomar el género del cine negro y adaptarlo a nuestro medio. El cine negro nunca pasa de moda porque su vigencia no es temporal sino moral. Es el más moral, el más ambiguo y el más democrático de todos los géneros. De ahí su triple atracción: así como nos podemos sentir culpables, también somos susceptibles de ser víctimas y, por qué no, cómplices. (Luis Ospina, “Mi último soplo”, 235-36)
3. It all begins with Borges...
But before we proceed any further, some background and some theory. Let's start with the origins of the detective figure in Latin American literature, and it is perhaps significant that the origin of this figure in Latin America is not just the classic detective figure, but that also the origin of the entangled and complicit post-Boom detective figure that we see in the novel and films we're looking at today. So, as it says here, it all begins with Jorge Luis Borges. In the 1940s, as an act of homage to Edgar Allan Poe, on the centenary of Poe's invention of the first modern detective, Borges decided to write a homage to Poe's famous story "The Murders in the Rue Morgue" of 1841 in which Poe’s detective C. Auguste Dupin first appeared.
3.1 “El jardín de los senderos que se bifurcan” (1941)
Borges published this as the now equally famous "El jardín de los senderos que se bifurcan" in 1941, exactly 100 years after Poe's masterpiece.
3.2 “La muerte y la brújula” (1942)
The next year, he wrote another in homage to Poe's "The Mystery of Marie Rogêt" of 1842, which became the story "La muerte y la brújula" of 1942.
3.3 “Abenjacán el Bojarí, muerto en su laberinto” (1951)
He had intended to rewrite  another in homage to Poe's famous "The Purloined Letter" of 1844 before the centenary of that story, but the timetable slipped and he didn't publish "Abenjacçan el Bojarí, muerto en su laberinto" until 1951.
3.4 Detective as “reader” – as proxy for the reader
In literary detective fiction from Poe's Auguste Dupin, Conan-Doyle's Sherlock Homes, to Agatha Christie's Poirot, the detective is in many ways a "reader" of clues, and his or her job is to piece together all the evidence and reconstruct a lost or shattered story.
3.4.1 Traditional detective as super-reader
Moreover, the traditional detective is a super-reader. The game is for you, humble reader, to pit your wits against the detective, to see if you can solve the crime before he does, or at least before he gathers together all the potential suspects in a theatrical-style ending and tells the narrative in order, restoring truth, morality and narrative to their proper places. But the detective is always one-better than you as a reader, s/he always reads the clues better than you can, and solves the narrative of the crime before you are able to. He is therefore a superior figure, someone who restores meaning to society, solving its crimes, putting order back where there was chaos. He has been described as a moral mediator of crime.
3.4.2 Borges’ inversion: criminal Red Scharlach weaves trap (fictional labyrinth) for Erik Lönnrot
Borges, in his most famous detective story, "La muerte y la brújula", which you should all read if you want to study this topic, inverts and parodies this figure. In that story, the master criminal Red Scharlach, weaves a trap for the master detective Erik Lönnrot, one in which the detective ends up caught in the spider's web of his own reasoning abilities. The criminal and the detective are already Doppelgängers, doubles, given that scharlach and rot, which appear in their names -- words which mean scarlet and red in German -- are synonyms of each other. But Scharlach arranges the clues to a series of murders in such a way that Lönnrot thinks he has found intricate patterns which foretell the date and time of the fourth and last murder. Believing he has anticipated events, he turns up at the predicted time and venue only to discover that he, the master detective, supreme believer in the powers of reason, is in fact destined to be the fourth victim.
3.5 “Lönnrot se creía un puro razonador, un Auguste Dupin” (“La muerte y la brújula”, p.148)
Borges explicitly tells us that "Lönnrot se creía un puro razonador, un Auguste Dupin".
3.6 Detective/reader gets caught in a fiction he believes to be true
But in fact the principle of reason turns out to be useless as a solution to solve the crimes of modernity, precisely because crime is intimately bound up with abstract reason.
3.7 Postmodern detective avant-la-lettre
Borges in a sense invents the postmodern detective we are looking at in our contemporary times, avant-la-lettre. This is because he calls into question the modern detective's faith in the power of pure reason, and shows up the complicity of the detective in the spider's web of crime. It is no longer possible for the postmodern detective to stand, God-like, above the crimes he wishes to solve. They exceed the powers of reason, and they exceed the ability of the detective to form a coherent narrative out of them, to calculate and relate the solution to the crime.
3.8 Why "post-Boom"?
Before I go any further, I should just briefly address why we call this topic "post-Boom detective fiction". Partly that's simply chronological: all the texts on SP13 are post-Boom. But it's also the case that this figure, in contrast to the Boom narratives' belief in exploring and revealing the deep structures of Latin American society, suggest ultimately that those deep structures cannot be known or revealed by the detective-reader. The detective is no longer a purveyor or discoverer of Truth. García Márquez himself marks this collapse of truth and revelation in his own post-Boom detective fiction which many of you may have read: Crónica de una muerte anunciada. In that novel, the detective-narrator returns to the scene of a notorious crime 27 years after it was committed, and tries to re-investigate it. He collects many testimonies, interviews the townsfolk, recovers the original magistrate's report, but discovers no new fact that explains the deep reasons behind the murder.
4. It all begins with Poe... “The Purloined Letter”
Having said that the postmodern, and hence the post-Boom, detective begins with Borges, it's clear from what I've said that in fact it all begins with...
4.1 Edgar Allan Poe’s tales of “ratiocination”
Edgar Alllan Poe's tales of "ratiocination" and his detective C. Auguste Dupin. Ratiocination is our reasoning ability, and it's clear, as I've already suggested, that the modern detective is set up as a figure of reason.
4.1.1 Detective C. Auguste Dupin
4.2 “The Murders in the Rue Morgue” (1841)
Here are the titles of Poe's three stories involving Aguste Dupin, to remind you, but let's just focus on "The Purloined Letter" for a moment, because it usefully sets out an important self-referential aspect of many detective stories that follow.
4.3 “The Mystery of Marie Rogêt” (1842)
4.4 “The Purloined Letter” (1844)
The word "purloined" means stolen, secreted away, and Poe's story is self-reflexive in that it refers to writing, a lost text, in its very title. Remember I said that the detective is a super-reader who must restore meaning and narrative to all the scattered clues of a disordered text? Well, Poe, at the very origin of the modern detective genre, seems to intuit this self-reflexivity of the detective's task, and allegorizes the lost text, and the lost meaning, that must be restored as a purloined letter on whose restoral to its rightful owner the very life of an aristocratic lady depends.
4.4.1 The importance of the stolen letter
The story concerns an aristocratic lady, a queen, we are told who has had a vital letter stolen from her boudoir, with the suspect being a Minister of her husband, the King. Given that it contains compromising information that could cost her her life, it is of the upmost importance that the letter should be returned to her. In desperation, after having searched the Minister's house, the French police contact amateur detective Dupin. Dupin finds the letter hidden in plain sight, on the mantlepiece in the Minister's apartment, which had been searched high-and-low by the police. The trick here is that the thief knew that the police would assume the letter to be hidden in an elaborate hiding place, so what better place to hide it but in plain sight where no-one would assume that could be the missing letter?
4.4.2 The loss / purloining of a text and its attempted recovery
The important point is that the story allegorizes the basic principle of detective fiction: the loss of a narrative, the loss of meaning, and its attempted recovery or restoration by the detective.
4.4.3 Recovery of lost text = restoration of meaning
This founds the entire genre of modern detective fiction, which concerns the recovery of a lost text and the restoration of meaning to a disturbed social order.
4.5 Lacan’s “Seminar on the Purloined Letter”
Some powerful theoretical guns have aimed their sights at Poe's "Purloined Letter", no less than Jacques Lacan and Jacques Derrida, the two masters of French psychoanalysis and deconstruction respectively. Lacan, in his famous "Seminar on the Purloined Letter", argues that…
4.5.1 Letter represents death (of Queen)
 the Letter represents the death (of the Queen), if revealed, it would have the power to kill her. Its contents are never revealed to us of course, and cannot be related, they must fall under the most primary of prohibitions. As such ...
4.5.2 Related to originary trauma / sexual transgression / primal scene (in the Queen's boudoir)
the letter is related to some originary trauma, possibly some sexual transgression, a primal scene that took place in the Queen's boudoir. Like the primal scene in psychoanalysis, the child's accidental observing of sex between his or her parents, for example, it becomes repressed into the unconscious.
4.5.3 Letter repeatedly "not seen" = disavowed (thus letter represents lack/castration which cannot be acknowledged under pain of death)
The letter, Lacan says, is repeatedly "not seen" in the story, it is disavowed, and so the letter represents lack or castration which cannot be acknowledged under the pain of death. Poe's story, he argues, demonstrates that the letter, the sign, the signifier, "insists" in the unconscious, that our entire psyche is fatally structured by and dictated by language, and the unconscious is a sign over which a fatal prohibition has fallen.
4.6 Derrida’s “Le Facteur de la vérité” (Truth's Postman / The Purveyor of Truth)
Jacques Derrida, on the other hand, in a biting critique of Lacan's interpretation of Poe's story, wrote an essay in which he accused Lacan of being "Le Facteur de la vérité", which is a pun, given that "facteur" in French means both postman and factor or purveyor. He is accusing Lacan, with his obsession with the letter sticking in the unconscious, of being "truth's postman", or "the purveyor of a (myth) of Truth".
4.6.1 Criticizes Lacan's reading for being fixated on the (veiled) "truth" of castration
I can't go into this complex argument in detail, but Derrida criticizes Lacan's reading for being fixated on the (veiled) "truth" of castration, and for believing that language and castration are the ultimate purveyors of the truth of the human psyche.
4.6.2 Lacan pays no attention to the "scene of writing" (of the letter, of Poe's trilogy)
He further criticizes Lacan for paying no attention to the "scene of writing" of the letter, which is also self-reflexively the "scene of writing" of Poe's detective trilogy itself. Derrida's reading is therefore much more postmodern than Lacan's, but I think they both point out useful things to us in interpreting post-Boom detective fiction. We will see that the loss of writing or narrative or a letter of one form or another is intimately bound up with crime and murder, and we will also see that there is no guarantee of truth in the postmodern detective's attempts to restore the missing narrative, and thus legibility, to the scene of the crime. Maybe the letter was meaningless in the first place, and meaning, the Law of the Father, patriarchal order based symbolically on castration, can no longer be restored (thankfully) in our postmodern societies.
5. It all begins with Sophocles... Image: Gustave Moreau, Oedipus and the Sphinx
OK, I've been telling an elaborate lie. Postmodern anti-detective fiction didn't all being with Borges, and nor did it all begin with Poe. In fact, it all began with Sophocles' Ancient Greek play Oedipus Rex.
5.1 Oedipus solves the riddle of the Sphinx
Oedipus' story, on which Freud based his entire theory of the modern psyche, concerns Oedipus' attempts to solve a crime. Prior to the main plot of the play, Oedipus had travelled to Thebes to help liberate the city from the scourge of the Sphinx who is devouring all travellers who cannot solve her riddle. He accidentally kills an old man on the way by knocking him off his chariot. He passes the Sphinx and manages to solve the riddle, whereupon the Sphinx throws herself from her rocky mountain and dies. Oedipus is proclaimed King of Thebes, especially as the previous king Laius has died.
5.2 Later he tries to discover the murderer of King Laius
Later, Oedipus tries to discover the murderer of the previous King Laius, and summons the visionary Tiresias, who eventually tells him that Oedipus himself is the criminal that Oedipus seeks. It gets worse, because later he discovers that not only was Laius the old man he killed on the way to Thebes, but also that Laius was his own father, and that he has unwittingly married and had children with his own mother, Laius's former wife whom he had taken as his own when he became King of Thebes. The detective thus turns out to be the criminal unwittingly. Let's look at Debra Moddlemog's explanation of why this story appears to be a detective story, but turns out to be what she calls an "anti-detective" story, but that we are calling anachronistically here, a postmodern detective story:
5.3 “Sophocles’ tragedy initially looks like a detective story, as does the antidetective story, in that its principal action begins with attending to a murder (albeit a rather tardy attending) and is spent upon investigating that crime. It becomes an antidetective story however when the pursuer turns out to be the pursued. Or, to straighten out the chronology here, Sophocles’ drama offers the narrative structure for one type of antidetective story, a type built upon three mythemes: the disclosure of a death (12); the investigation surrounding that death (13); and the investigator’s recognition of his or her complicity (15). The first two mythemes form the structural core of the detective tale, but when the third is deployed, we have moved into the realm of the antidetective.” (Moddelmog, Readers and Mythic Signs p.64)
6. Perder es cuestión de método (novel, 1997; film, 2004)
Having set out the framework for undersanding the postmodern detective story, and its elaboration in noir and neo-noir, let's now turn to the texts I'm using by way of example today. The first is the novel, and more or less faithful film rendition of it, Perder es cuestión de método, published by Santiago Gamboa in 1997.
6.1 Plot
6.1.1 Journalist and private detective Víctor Silanpa
The plot concerns journalist and private detective Víctor Silanpa, whom we saw in the opening clip documenting the facts of the murder.
6.1.2 Called on by police captain Aristófanes Moya to solve mystery of gruesomely staked body
He has been called on by the police captain Aristófanes Moya to solve the mystery of the gruesomely staked body that we saw also in that opening clip. The body has been put on display on the side of a lake and reservoir near Bogotá, and seems to be a warning.
6.1.3 Three rival strong men are trying to gain control of a piece of prime real estate by the side of a lake near Bogotá
Three rival strong men are in dispute over the piece of prime real estate land, that could be developed at great profit, by the side of this lake. They are:
Marco Tulio Esquilache, concejal
The first is Marco Tulio Esquilache, who is a local politician, conejal or town councillor as we would say in the UK. He has political control over planning decisions.
Vargas Vicuña, constructor
The second is Vargas Vicuña, who is a powerful real-estate developer, in cahoots with the banking industry which finances most real-estate development in Colombia, a major source of capitalist profit.
Heliodoro Tiflis, mafioso
Finally, there is Heliodoro Tiflis, a mafia man running a powerful cartel.
6.1.4 The Nudist club and "bare life"
The lake is the site of a nudist club for high-class Bogotanos or rolos, and the owner of the land, Casiodoro Pereira Antúnez, was a founder of the club, who on his death donated the land to the club. However, the deeds, symbolically called escrituras in Spanish, have disappeared, and it seems that possession of the deeds would infer title of the property over the person who is able to wield them, or at least the escrituras combined with political power and bribery, would allow the possessor of the deeds to control the land.
As part of his investigations into the crime of the staked corpse, Silanpa, together with a young woman called Quica, a nightclub sex worker whose older brother was murdered in a shootout in a poor neighbourhood to the south of Bogotá and who decides to help out Silanpa, manages to get into the nudist club under false pretenses, in order to assess the principal actors in this tale of intrigue.
I shall later examine the themes of biopolitics and necropolitics underlying much of Colombian neo-noir, and this film is no exception. For now it is sufficient to say that biopolitics is a mode of governance the manages humans as biological entities with life processes that the state needs to control, manipulate and, in the best of cases, care for in order to govern. It is no accident that these novels and films emphasize the politics of the body. Although we might believe that here we are viewing a form of “bare life”, the human body stripped down to its biological vulnerability, it is important to realize that the naked bodies in this novel and film are far from “bare”: they are the élite of Bogotá, and they remain clothed in power and money, even as they remove their clothes within the safe confines of a luxury establishment and of the class that they represent.

6.1.5 Deeds [escrituras] have disappeared -- different people possess them at different times
You will of course recognize the basic structure of the detective fiction that we examined in Poe's "Purloined Letter" in this tale of missing deeds (escrituras). Here it is not a purloined letter, but purloined escritura, writing itself, or the capture of land and nature in the legal structure of the state, which has been stolen, and his being desperately fought over by the capitalist interests of the country, symbolically figured in the politician, the capitalist financier, and the mafioso. As a first step, solving the crime of the staked man's murder means recovering the purloined escritura, the lost narrative of the law and its legal systems.
6.2 The postmodern detective: investigative journalist?
Silanpa, remember, is an investigative journalist, not a traditional private detective. He is thus already an unlikely figure for a detective, given that journalists are usually more interested in "a good story" than in the truth of a crime, and Silanpa has been given this case precisely as a potentially lucrative story for his newspaper, a bribe to get him to write a speech for the chief of police who has employed him. This is one of the ways in which Silanpa calls into question the detective as purveyor of truth, or truth's postman, as Derrida put it. Journalists obey other interests than the truth, and already the "truth" that they reveal is ultimately beholden to commercial interests. If a story is likely to damage the advertising revenue of the newspaper, or other outlet, then it will be quickly changed or suppressed.
6.3 Spatio-temporal dysphasia
The novel, and film version, concern the struggle over profitable land, i.e. a struggle to control the spatial basis of capitalism, its ability to extract profit from land by subjecting it to the abstract forces of exchange. At the same time, it concerns temporal dysphasia, which means the disordering of temporality, the temporal havoc that finance capitalism wreaks on our lives and which I have discussed in other lectures under the rubric of velocity and speed-up, increased turnover of goods and information, and loss of temporal depth or history.
6.3.1 Detective's relationship to time
There is a handout that accompanies this lecture, which contains quotations from the novel, although I've put the ones I'm referring to here as well. The first one illustrates this sense of temporal dysphasia that is of course disastrous for the figure of the detective. A detective needs to work intimately with chronology. He or she is initially presented with a disordered chronology, a set of scattered clues divorced from the coherent temporality of a narrative that would make sense of them, and his job is to work out the chronology in order to restore the narrative -- and the meaning -- to the social text.  Here, we can see this detective losing control of temporal relations from the very outset of the story:
14: (Víctor Silanpa): Al llegar al tercer puente miró el reloj y vio que eran casi las cinco: «Mónica debe estar furiosa», se dijo. Aceleró hasta la avenida 127 y luego bajó hacia Niza reprochándose ser como era: alguien perdido en el tiempo, incapaz de cumplir con una cita, como si las coordenadas del reloj fueran un lenguaje ajeno a su vida.
6.3.2 Fernando Guzmán, 'mad' former editor of national newspaper
Silanpa's mentor in the film is the previous editor of the newspaper he works for, Fernando Guzmán. Guzmán had gone mad, as a result of the increasing complexity of the information pouring in every night to his newspaper, and the need to make sense of it in order to tell a coherent national news narrative. He had had a nervous breakdown as a result of his incapacity to make sense of all this chaotic information, of the rapidly expanding networks of political, economic and social corruption, and had been interned in an asylum. In this asylum he has been forbidden, for his own health, from reading the daily newspaper. However, as a concession, as we'll see in this clip from the film version, corresponding to the quotation from p.30 of the novel on the handout, Guzmán has been allowed to read back copies of the newspapers starting from the day he first entered the clinic a few years earlier. Old news, already history, is judged to be no longer detrimental to his health. He reads this news like a fictional narrative, as he puts it here "la historia por entregas", serialized history, history read not as truth, but as fiction. Look out for his mention of a major traumatic historical event for Colombia, the siege of the Ministry of Justice, "la toma del Palacio de Justicia", by guerrillas financed by Pablo Escobar in 1985. Guzmán has just got to that point in history, which he is reading like a novel, and Silanpa taunts him by telling him that he cannot possibly imagine what comes next. Guzmán tells him not to give him a spoiler, "no me mate la intriga"...

6.3.3 Toma del Palacio de Justicia Nov 6th 1985
The siege of the Palacio de Justicia took place on November 6th 1985, right at the symbolic heart of the nation on the Plaza Bolívar. If you've seen the Netflix series on Pablo Escobar, you may know about this. It was the most audacious and bloody action of the former Colombian guerrilla organization the M19, probably financed by Pablo Escobar in an attempt to destroy evidence that could be used to extradite him and members of his cartel to the USA. The army over-reacted and stormed the building, leading to...
M-19 - 76 dead, including 11 judges 10 canteen workers "disappeared"
76 deaths, including 11 justices. There is also, still unresolved today, the case of 10 canteen workers who were seen being led away by the military, but subsequently disappeared completely.
6.3.4 Exactly one week later: Armero tragedy, Nov 13th 1985
What Silanpa is referring to when he says "no se imagina lo que viene después" is the fact that, exactly a week after this huge crisis that rocked and threatened to destroy the most powerful institutions of the country, a natural tragedy hit Colombia, on November 13th 1985. The town of Armero was engulfed and destroyed by a flood of hot, melted ice and mud pouring down the slopes of the Nevado del Ruiz volcano as a consequence of an eruption.
Nevado del Ruiz volcano - mudslide 25,000 killed
The town was destroyed and around 25,000 people killed, buried alive in the hot mud, which quickly hardened to the consistency of concrete. Armero represented yet another national trauma for Colombia, one which somehow became a "naturalization" of the crime and violence wracking the country.
Displacement of social violence onto an imaginary of "natural" catastrophe is one of the main themes of Luis Ospina's foundational neo-noir film Soplo de vida
The film Soplo de vida by Luis Ospina, explicitly addresses the displacement of social violence onto an imaginary of "natural" catastrophe in his aforementioned foundational neo-noir film Soplo de vida. In this film, many of the principal characters of the film turn out to have been themselves refugees from Armero, and the film begins with archival footage of the aftermath of the eruption, suggesting the foundational status of this event, metaphorically substituting for the corruption-fuelled political violence afflicting the country.
7. Noir from biopolitics to necropolitics: La sangre y la lluvia (film: Jorge Navas, 2009)

7.1 Plot
This film is set in Bogotá, like Perder es cuestión de método, over 24 hours, much of which is a rain-soaked night and following day.

 It concerns the investigation by taxi-driver Jorge, to uncover the perpetrator of the recent murder of his brother William. Bogotá is divided into rival cartels of taxi-drivers, who run the lucrative business, and control the streets and even the airwaves of the city at night.

She meets taxi-driver Jorge after he has just been beaten up by members of a rival taxi cartel. Ángela finds herself obliged to attend to his wounds and take him to A&E, and slowly gets sucked into the dark intrigues of the rival taxi cartels.
7.2 Thoroughly noir aesthetic
The film is infused with a thoroughly noir aesthetic.
7.2.1 Flow of substances, sexual desire, criminal underworld, explored through metaphor of "transurban" network
It concerns flows of substances, unfulfilled sexual desire and the criminal underworld explored through the metaphor of a "transurban" network of taxis that roam the city at night.

7.3 From biopolitics to necropolitics
As mentioned earlier, I want to use this film to introduce one more framework which could be useful for your reading of what kinds of shifts are charted in post-Boom detective fiction and the neo-noir turn. This is the shift from the biopolitics of classic noir to the necropolitics of neo-noir. I shall explain to what extent noir generally is a deeply biopolitical genre -- a genre deeply imbued with the problem of managing politically the life-processes of the population. But it’s important to state that the shift from biopolitics to necropolitics is not a dualism, i.e. necropolitics, a term associated with the politics of death, is not opposed to biopolitics or the politics of life, it is instead the radicalization of biopolitics, of a branch within biopolitics. Necropolitics in this sense is a specialized part of the more generalized term biopolitics.
7.3.1 The meaning of biopolitics in these films
Biopolitics in classic noir
Classic noir has always had an obscure biopolitics. Biopolitics, as defined by Foucault, is politics designed to administer the human life processes, and "put life in order". Classic noir explores the disruption to the biopolitical order, and it is itself biopolitical to the extent that it demands restitution of the disrupted social or patriarchal order, the bios of family, polis and state, through the agency of the police.
Film stages the confluence of violence, urban imaginaries, flows of cocaine and money that feed these imaginaries, and lines of transport criss-crossing the city
La sangre y la lluvia, as its title evocatively suggests, stages the metaphorical mixing of the fluids of the body with the rain-soaked city streets  The body is directly evoked at every turn in its visceral dramas. The film stages the confluence of violence, urban imaginaries that are in many ways more intense than the disappointing reality, the flows of cocaine and money that feed these imaginaries, and lines of transport by which bodies criss-cross the city. In the most fundamental of ways, then, it is thoroughly bio-political. But there are competing forces in contemporary urban life, forces which pull us away from embodied existence into a world of abstractions, flows, what in previous lectures I termed “the space of flows” following Castells, and violent forces that attack and manipulate us as bodily beings. Let’s look at a theoretical quotation that sums up this idea:
La materialidad histórica de la ciudad en su conjunto sufre así una fuerte devaluación, su “cuerpo-espacio” pierde peso en función del nuevo valor que adquiere su tiempo, “el régimen general de la velocidad” [Virilio]. No es difícil ver aquí la conexión que enlaza esa descorporización de la ciudad con el cada día más denso flujo de la imágenes devaluando y hasta sustituyendo el intercambio de experiencias entre las gentes. (Jesús Martín-Barbero, “Urbanías y ciudadanías“, Al sur de la modernidad p.130)
In this quotation, Colombian media and urban theorist Jesús Martín-Barbero, in his book Al sur de la modernidad, describes the decorporealizaiton of the city that occurs as the flow of enticing media images seem to dissolve the time and space of the city.
The body becomes a material theatre for the interplay of biopolitical and necropolitical forces
At the same time, these novels and films insist on pulling us back towards the suppressed body and its life processes which lies at the heart of modern biopolitical regimes, creating a dialectical interplay. In this sense, the body as locus for the transfusion of pleasure, pain, and other intensities becomes a material theatre for the interplay of biopolitical and necropolitical forces in neo-noir films and texts.
7.3.2 Necropolitics
Necropolitics is an intensification of biopolitics. As the state recedes from care for the welfare of the population in the neoliberal city of the 2000s and 2010s, a politics of death, one that seeks to deploy death as a political tool of control, rushes in to fill the void. The most important theorist of necropolitics in the global south is Achille Mbembe, and to give you a sense of what this term means, I reproduce a quotations from his famous essay on "Necropolitics".
An important feature of the age of global mobility is that military operations and the exercise of the right to kill are no longer the sole monopoly of states, and the “regular army” is no longer the unique modality of carrying out these functions. The claim to ultimate or final authority in a particular political space is not easily made. Instead, a patchwork of overlapping and incomplete rights to rule emerges, inextricably superimposed and tangled, in which different de facto juridical instances are geographically interwoven and plural allegiances, asymmetrical sovereignties, and enclaves abound. (p. 31)
Necropolitics in this sense is not the collapse of the sovereign right to decree who will live and who will die. Instead, it is the multiplication and intensification of those rights formerly attributable only to the sovereign, but now attributable to this patchwork of overlapping and incomplete rights to rule. This is precisely the scenario that emerges in La sangre y la lluvia.
7.3.3 Absence of state, police
Despite it being a neo-noir detective film, in La sangre y la lluvia no police make an appearance until the final sequences of the film, when it is far too late. The state and its agents of control have lost their relevance, they have been substituted by a multitude of competing, necropolitical micro-sovereignties.
Taxi drivers take on characteristics of private security foce: privatized policing
In this film, it is the taxi drivers who, somewhat incongruously, take on characteristics of a private security force filling in for the absent state police. The taxi-driver cartels represent a form of privatized policing, as corrupt as the sate police, but beholden only to each mafia boss, infused by the logic of necropolitics as a way of controlling flows of money.
8. The fall of the detective
Let's now return to Perder es cuestión de método and look at the fall and corruption of the detective in this film.
8.1 No longer an agent for morality
As already mentioned, in Perder, the detective is no longer an agent for morality, no longer what Glen Close calls an agent for "the moral mediation of crime" ("The Detective is Dead: Long Live the novela negra"). This begins, as I said, with the fact that this detective is not by profession a detective, but an investigative journalist, already "untado" and "involucrado" in the words of the theme song by Aterciopelados that we saw in the first clip.
8.2 Silanpa undertakes private detective work on behalf of jealous wives
At the beginning of the story, we observe Silanpa making a bit of money on the side by undertaking private detective work on behalf of jealous wives. And we also see him corruptly making money from both parties in these adulterous affairs: accepting the wives' money to go after their wayward husbands and catch them in flagrante, and then accepting payment from the adulterous husbands in order to suppress the evidence gathered. From the outset, then, hie operates to double standards, as in this quotation (see handout).
8.2.1 33: Alistó la cámara y abrió disparando golpes de flash y gritando «¡Nadie se mueva, policíaaa!» […]. —Sé que es mi mujer la que lo manda y por lo tanto podemos hablar con franqueza: ¿Cuánto? —¿Cuánto qué? —No nos hagamos los pendejos.
8.3 Detective as loser
Furthermore, the very title tells us that this detective is a loser, a perdedor. The title comes from novelist Luis Sepúlveda, a quotation given us on p.45.
8.3.1 45: «Perdí. Siempre perdí. No me irrita ni preocupa. Perder es una cuestión de método»: Luis Sepúlveda.
This quotation neatly inverts the reason that according to Edgar Allen Poe was the mainstay of the modern detective in his tales of the reasoning mind, "Tales of Ratiocination"… The method of reason here is no longer applicable to the solving of a crime. Instead, reason and method are inverted, and become forms of losing, not forms of winning.
8.4 No longer an agent for truth in postmodern society
As well as no longer standing as an agent of restitution of a moral order, the postmodern detective can of course no longer act as an agent of truth, because truth has become an uncertain category in a world of fake news, "stories" rather facts or truths.
8.4.1 Loss of ultimate signifiers
Postmodernism is often defined as a loss of faith in Grand Narratives of Enlightenment and modernity, and of transcendental signifiers such as Truth, Justice, Emancipation, Revolution.
8.4.2 Note names of characters suggesting classical grandeur, degraded in present
Notice how the names of the characters incongruously point to classical grandeur in a degraded present: not to somehow ennoble that present, but precisely to suggest the collapse of those grand classical or heroic narratives. Marco Tulio, Heliodoro Tiflis, Aristófanes Moya, Casiodoro Pereira Antúnez...
8.4.3 Loss of political categories that should stand in opposition to each other
Like all good detectives, Víctor Silanpa has a semi-comic sidekick, called Estupiñán. Every now and then he comically asks a naïve or unanswerable political question which floors Silanpa and leaves us with a sense of the collapse of even the political mediation of collective agency that should be the mainstay of democracy. This one from the end of the novel is typical:
315: [Estupiñán:] —¿Puedo hacerle una preguntica, jefe? Es que hay una vaina que nunca he entendido. […] ¿Cuál es la diferencia entre estado de sitio y estado de derecho?
What kind of political imagination could collapse the distinction between the rule of law and the state of siege? The two things are only compatible in a world in which the rule of law has collapsed into the suspension of law..., a state we associate only with dictatorships.

9.2 Circulation of escrituras parallel to circulation of dead body
Finally, the circulation of the escrituras in this novel is parallel to the circulation of the dead body we saw illustrated in my first clip, and which Guzmán is also a sign, a form of writing. I'll leave you to find out the solution to the crime, and the role played by the transportation of this body around the country in this obscure tale of corruption and intrigue.
9.2.1 As in Marx, capital = undead labour
Suffice it to say that the criculation of the body and of the escrituras in parallel strongly evoke Karl Marx's contention that finance capital should be understood as undead labour. It is the accumulation of the dead labour of all the past workers, re-animated like a zombie by the capitalist, who puts that accumulated dead labour back into circulation in a way that feeds off the living bodies of today's workers, like some undead vampire.
9.2.2 Capital as disavowal of its own cruelty; Cain prospers through disavowal of murder of Abel
Capital is built on death and has a powerful necropolitcs. It is a disavowal of its own cruelty. The murderer Cain prospers and enriches himself through his disavowal of his slaying of Abel. In the final analysis, this necropolitical landscape of capitalism is, I believe, the ultimate horizon of critique contained in our contemporary postmodern detective fictions and the Latin American appropriation of the neo-noir genre.
