3. Dystopian cities
The principal literary antecedent of La sonámbula, is, as I mentioned, Ricardo Piglia’s cyberpunk novel of 1992, La ciudad ausente, which hardly surprising since Piglia co-wrote the film’s script. Many elements overlap between the novel and the film: a cyborg story-telling woman-machine in La ciudad ausente is referred to explicitly as a future Eve, an ‘Eva futura’, while the female protagonist of La sonámbula, whose visions are captured directly on a giant computer screen and seem capable of foretelling the future, is actually named Eva; both plots are set in a near dystopian future, one in which personal and collective memory and identity have imploded along with nature itself, and one in which a post-dictatorial technocratic state has fused itself with the libidinal control systems of globalised capital.
The repressive overgrown cityscape of Buenos Aires in the film follows in the tradition "old future" portrayed in Ridley Scott's film Blade Runner in which present, past and future seem to have collapsed, or in which the future simulates the past postmodern-style. While in Blade Runner the cityscape of Los Ángeles in 2019 is a baroque fusion of the futuristic and the simulation of ancient pyramids, perhaps Mayan, together with an imploded Dickensian vision of industrialization run riot, in La sonámbula, the cityscape of Buenos Aires in 2010 -- the bicentenary of Argentine Independence -- fuses churches and cathedrals with a jungle of elevated highways and skyscrapers which signify the futurist city not as future, but as citation of old sci-fi movies, principally Fritz Lang's Metropolis of 1926. The future is thus filtered through a past cinematic imaginary, an inversion which is aided by the fact that the entire futuristic plot of the film is shot in black-and- white, whereas the flashbacks to the past or present -- the visions of the protagonist Eva Rey -- are shot in full technicolour. The subtitle of the film is "recuerdos del futuro", "memories of the future".
3.1 "Futurist" city as citation of old sci-if movies, filtered through past cinematic imaginary
I shall now show a couple of clips to illustrate this, starting with the opening of Blade Runner so that you can see how consciously La sonámbula cites that film. In both films, the eye imagery sets in train a self-reflexive focus on the prosthetics of vision -- the eye as interface between the organic and the technological, the visual media as generators of a libidinal economy of prohibition and desire and as manufacturers of memory.

3.2 Lefebvrian vision of the consumption of organic time (Lefebvrian = relating to the work of urban philosopher Henri Lefebvre)
In the world of La sonámbula, the Lefebvrian vision of the urban consumption of organic time, the compulsion to subsume all vestiges of natural rhythms and cycles into synthetic time and life-cycles of the commodity, has been taken to a nightmare extreme (see the still on the front page of this lecture, showing a postindustrial commodity graveyard). This has a dual focus: that of ecological disaster and the literal loss of that which links us to organic time cycles, memory itself. In the film, the two are intimately linked. The plot of the film concerns a future in which a huge industrial accident, a probably deliberate explosion at an experimental chemical factory in a popular district of Buenos Aires, has left 300,000 people with wiped out memories and hence no sense of their own identity or relationship to the past. All of the affected people in the film bear a highly visible mark, either on the face or on some other part of the body such as forearm or chest, a mark which becomes a substitute for identity and even erotic fetish within the film's self-consciously condensed dream logic.

3.3 Memory
In many ways memory -- or rather, memory and amnesia -- is the key theme of La sonámbula. In an iterview, Piglia explains the importance of this theme:
3.3.1 Piglia
"Nosotros pensamos en la idea de una película de ciencia ficción en el sentido de que hay una idea, una especulación sobre el presente que se traslada al futuro. Tomamos la problemática de la memoria, las cosas que se recuerdan o no en la Argentina, y lo convertimos en un universo en el futuro, donde ese es el problema que define la vida. Quiero decir que en última instancia la ciencia ficción no es otra cosa que usar el futuro para hablar del presente. Entonces la película es una película de ciencia ficción porque funciona de esa manera, porque está puesta en el futuro, y por lo tanto, como pasa con la ciencia ficción, es muy política (la ciencia ficción es un género muy político casi de manera natural, porque se trata siempre de sociedades donde se está produciendo siempre algún tipo de conflicto)." Entrevista - http://www.mabuse.com.ar/mabuse/piglia.htm .
3.3.2 Conflation of technological imaginary and the unconscious
Throughout the film cinema, or rather televisual culture more broadly, is self-reflexively figured as prosthetic memory, or simulated memory. The central computer screen in the film is represented as a giant cinema screen, with images displayed in colour as against the black-and-white of the rest of the film. The machine records telepathically, interfacing directly with the mind and reproducing thought televisually. In particular, as in La ciudad ausente, the machine records dreams, so that the film figures a total conflation of the spheres of the technological imaginary with the unconscious.
Beller
In this, it projects forward contemporary analyses of the technological mediation of personal and collective imaginaries, of which the summary given by Jonathan Beller is fairly representative. He writes:
"Metz argues that 'cinema is a technique of the imaginary' [...]. However, the scope of today's (counter)revolution [...] emerges from a reversal of these very terms: the imaginary is a technique of cinema, or rather, of mediation generally. Such a reversal de-ontologizes the unconscious and further suggests that the unconscious is cinema's product [...]" p.64
Jonathan L. Beller, "Kino-I, Kino-world" in Nicholas Mirzoeff, ed., The Visual Culture Reader.
3.3.3 Programmed amnesia
But of course these programmed memories are a form of programmed amnesia, designed to replace memory -- ultimately an experiment in social programming. A couple of clips will help to situate the film's representation of these processes. The first is in the primary evaluation centre of the Centre of Psychobiological Research, where the amnesiacs are brought to initiate their re-insertion into society. Here the rhetoric of recuperating memory is bound up with a primitive Huxleyan reprogramming with normative memories through televisual technology. The second shows "El Duke", a kind of substitute father for Ariel, who explains the politics behind this literalized vision of the postmodern consumption of memory -- in which it should not be too hard to see barely veiled references to the dictatorship. Finally, I'll show the mad scientist and his computer screen, the sinister Dr Gazzar. Note the way black-and-white and colour are deployed even here to separate dream representations and the "real" (which itself may be a simulation).

3.4 Ecological disaster (desert of the real)
The consumption of organic time and memory is logically extended in the film to the total consumption of nature. The countryside outside Buenos Aires is literally a post-industrial desert, a Borgesian-cum-Baudrillardian "desert of the real", consumed literally by industrial chaos as well as through compulsive simulation. Even the migration of birds as organic marker of natural time cycles has become disturbed as we are told that the huge flocks of birds just circle and circle the desert without orientation, as we see in the following two clips.
