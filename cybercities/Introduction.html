1. Introduction
This lecture is intended both for the Cinematic City topic or for the Post-Dictatorship Argentine Cinema topic.
The films today are both from Argentina, made at the end of the 1990s, and both fall into the genre of dystopian science fiction. In both films, the megacity is projected into a future in which all the trends of the post-dictatorial, neoliberal present of end-of-millennium Argentina are hypostatized into a future in which nature has been thoroughly consumed by industry, where political amnesia has become the norm and is in fact promulgated by the State, and where the paradoxical topographies of the megalopolis and the Network Society which we discussed last week have fundamentally transformed people's lives and social relations.
Temporality and spatiality are also thoroughly warped in these films. The past seems to have collapsed into the future, and the interconnections of megapolitan space have become so enormous that they explode our ability to map them cognitively.
Being Argentine, these films are fairly intellectual. Moebius was made under the auspices of a film school, by students and teachers, and the other, La sonámbula, is based on a film script written by the Argentine novelist Ricardo Piglia, itself loosely based on his highly complex urban novel La ciudad ausente (1992). Mixed into these films is a whole tradition of cultural thought on urban labyrinths and the power of filmic simulation to become a metaphor for our age, from Borges, through Adolfo Bioy Casares, to Piglia himself.

1.3.1 Urban life and dissolution of boundaries: moulding the body into forms of manufacture/consumption
Likewise, the new and radical forms of interconnection encouraged by urban existence are often understoood in terms of the dissolution of boundaries between the human and the machinic, with the city being understood as a technology for moulding the body to the forms of industrial manufacture and consumption.
Moebius is set almost entirely in the Buenos Aires underground system ("subte"), with all the transported bodies seen as part of some gigantic urban machine, a network of incalculable proportions. 1.3.2 Cybernetic culture: collapse of distinction between spheres of industry/ technology and representation
Since the 1980s, the massive emergence of cybernetic cultures has collapsed at its very base the distinction between the technological sphere of industry and the ideological sphere of representations.
That may seem a little strange, but think of our media society and Internet society. If representations have become the ‘raw material’ on which the cyber economy does its work, and if all transformations of the material world can be coded in terms of a problem of information processing, then cultural forms such as film, which was originally an imaginary nexus between the individual and the collective in the burgeoning metropoles of the 1930s, 40s and 50s, now take on a new almost allegorical character in relation to the power embedded in forms of representation.
This is definitely the case of the two films we'll be looking at today.
1.4 Argentine sci-fi, cyberpunk and regimes of simulation: the cyborg
One of the films, La sonámbula, uses the sci-fi figure of the cyborg and cyberpunk, and I just want to explain this figure briefly.
Argentina's experience under the dictatorship of 1976 to 1982 was one of erased bodies, programmed identities and processed memories. This allows for an appropriation of US sci-fi cyborg mythologies and cyberpunk in a way that focuses sharply on the power structures inherent within the new global regimes of power and knowledge.
The cyborg is generally understood, following Donna Haraway's famous Cyborg Manifesto, as an ambiguous technological artefact, part human, part machine, which disturbs boundaries—between cultures, between the organic and the artificial. For Haraway, we are all cyborgs -- we are all addressed by technology, bathed in technology, perceiving the world through technology, with prosthetic voices and ears in the form of telephones and eyes in the form of computer and television screens.
1.4.1 Dictatorship 1976-82: 30,000 disappearances
Nostalgic narratives of repression and liberation, congealed around repressive fantasies of sexual difference
It's also the case that these films tend to activate nostalgic narratives of repression and liberation, congealed around repressive fantasies of sexual difference, fixing the "rhizomatic" operations of power into binary structures congealed around fantasies of sexual difference.This should really come as no surprise, since we know that power and simulation are caught in a tight bind, and that fetish identities such as those built around sexual difference depend on disavowal-as-simulation and promote the proliferation of simulacra. In this sense, the films represent something like a transitional aesthetic, between conceptualizations of power and liberation as mutually reinforcing, and the idea of more modulated processes of control in the Network Society.
 
1.4.2 Specific anxieties surrounding dissolution of collective identities and collective memory
The presence of the cyborg in Latin American culture retains the features that Haraway outlines, but often renders them deeply citational, because they are so clearly appropriated from US Sci-Fi. Latin American cyborgs also seem to condense rather specific anxieties surrounding the dissolution of collective identities and collective memory.
1.4.3 Erasure of nation as space of collective agency and memory
This connects historically to the experience of colonization on the one hand and, on the other, to the erasure of the nation as a space of collective agency and memory under globalization and neoliberalism.
1.4.4 Spatio-temporal breach of the Latin American megalopolis
Another salient feature of Latin American cyborgs would seem to be their confusion of temporalities. They seem to collapse temporal frames and phenomena as apparently disparate as nineteenth-century Independence and twenty-first century memory implants in the case of La sonámbula. In this way they represent the confusion of temporalities, of the old and the new, at the heart of the Latin American megalopolis, where extreme poverty and quasi rural lifestyles can co-exist with some of the most advanced banking and telecommunications infrastructure in the service of global capitalism.
