4. Paradoxical (urban) topologies
4.1 Moebius as exploration of the new spatial and temporal topographies
Moebius is an exploration of the new spatial and temporal topographies that emerge both in the megalopolis at the turn of the millennium and within increasingly interconnected systems and spheres of everyday life on both the local and the global scale, registering the growing complexity and interconnectivity of Castells' Network Society. The film’s approach to these themes is in line with the Borgesian tradition, which is to say, largely allegorical and philosophical, with one particular network – that of the underground or subte in Buenos Aires – standing for wider social processes.
4.1.1 Complexity and interconnectivity of the Network Society
4.1.2 Traditional theme of urban narrative, partic. surrealist (Nadja, Paysan de Paris, Benjamin's Passagen, Cortázar's Paris in Rayuela)
This is in many ways quite a traditional theme of urban narrative, particularly of the surrealist ilk, stretching back to Nadja, Paysan de Paris, Benjamin’s Passagenwerk, and of course Cortázar’s doubled cities of Paris and Buenos Aires in Rayuela.
A.J. Deutsch, "A Subway Named Möbius"
The film is based on a little known short story by US astronomer A.J. Deutsch entitled "A Subway Named Möbius" (1950). The film concerns the mysterious disappearance on the Buenos Aires underground of one complete train with 30 or 40 passengers. The train simply cannot be found, although the odd distant rumblings are heard even when the entire network is supposedly shut down, and the system registers the passage of phantom trains which disrupt the signalling and point mechanisms.
4.2 Emphasis on maps and machines suggestive of Borgesian challenge to rational systems of measurement
Gustavo Mosquera, the film’s director, has stated that he intentionally expanded the representation of the network by adding new station names to supplement existing ones ‘in order to suggest the idea of a future underground railway with a larger extension than the present one’.
Unlike the surrealist tradition, but in line perhaps with Benjamin, the emphasis on maps and machines in this film is suggestive not only of a Borgesian challenge to rational systems of measurement, indeed to the concept of a fixed Euclidian space in which we live out our lives, but also, if we take this to be a metaphor, a breakdown of cognitive mapping in the increasing complexity of urban networks.
4.2.1 Cognitive mapping
Cognitive mapping is a concept used by geographers to name the internal representations we make for ourselves of the spaces which we traverse in everyday life. As the complexity of these systems increases, these maps seem to break down. In the film, the interconnectivity of the system has, it appears, reached incalculable and unmeasurable proportions.
The urban space of the megalopolis, let alone the global space of multinational capitalism, becomes fundamentally unrepresentable
This would then suggest that there are two contradictory impulses at work in the figurative apparatus of this film: on the one hand, a spatial complexity which challenges the rationality of Euclidean mapping and which introduces the protagonist to a concept of the in-finite as a form of liberation, as we shall see; and on the other, a sense of the conceptual and social paralysis that accompanies our immersion in the unmappable new topologies and temporalities of the network society.
Disruption of Euclidian space
4.2.2 Interconnectivity of the system has reached incalculable proportions
Despite constant searching, the missing train in the film simply cannot be found, although the odd distant rumblings are heard even when the entire network is supposedly shut down, and the system registers the passage of phantom trains which disrupt the signalling and point mechanisms. Since a new perimeter line was added, no-one quite seems to understand the interconnectivity of the system, and amidst the scandal of the disappearance and telephone calls to the Director General’s office from concerned relatives of the disappeared, a young topologist, Daniel Pratt, is drafted in to investigate. Pratt discovers that the original plans of the perimeter extension line have disappeared from the archive of the Ministry of Public Works, having been illicitly borrowed by one Hugo Mistein, a brilliant former professor of Pratt’s in the field of topology who retired several years earlier. Pratt manages to locate Mistein’s flat and, with the help of a young girl, Abril, is let into the recently abandoned apartment. There he finds the missing plans together with many scribbled notes and formulae, in amongst which the words ‘NÚCLEO MOEBIUS’ stand out. Pratt then attempts to explain to the railway authorities – a triumvirate of men strongly suggestive of a military junta – his conjectures about the way the topology of the network has disrupted conventional Euclidean space.
