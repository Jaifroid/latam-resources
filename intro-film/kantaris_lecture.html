Key issues in the study of Ibero-American Film

Instead, I want to set film form in the context of key issues to consider when studying film from the Iberian peninsula and Latin America, with an emphasis on what happens when film moves between cultures and across lines of power and cultural difference. Here are some of the key issues I aim to touch upon.
The approach to the real (documentary, fiction, mockumentary)
Representation and power: filming subaltern subjects
Globalization and the mass media
The migration of cultures and identities

Illustrate with a Mexican-Cuban film: ¿Quién diablos es Juliette?  (dir. Carlos Marcovich, 2000)

I shall illustrate my points with a Mexican-Cuban film, which lies unstably on the boundary between documentary and fiction, ¿Quién diablos es Juliette? (2000).

¿Quién diablos es Juliette? was credited at the time of its appearance with being the most locally and internationally successful Mexican "documentary" to date in terms of ticket sales and distribution. It was filmed in Cuba, Mexico and the United States, and flamboyantly cuts across national territories and mediatic genres in a way which defies easy categorization.

The film follows the parallel lives (although the parallels are self-reflexively constructed within the film) between a Cuban teenager, Yuliet Ortega, who lives in a marginal district of La Habana, and a Mexican model who spends time in New York, Fabiola Quiroz. The director and Fabiola had first met Yuliet in La Habana in 1993 while they were on a video shoot for a commissioned music video, Benny Ibarra’s hit single Tonto corazón:

Meditation on visual culture

The film interweaves the story of the two women’s lives, as told through interviews with friends and family as well as the protagonists, with a highly playful and self-reflexive visual style. It features a bewildering away of forms of visual culture, from documentary filmmaking, fiction film interpreted as feminist pedagogy, the work of the Italian-American artist Francisco Clemente, and photography. It is in many ways a reflexive feast of visual culture which allows us to think about the social role and function of such culture, as well as its location between national cultures.

Story told through editing

The story is largely constructed through flamboyant editing, with the characters often appearing to be in “dialogue” across impossibly vast territorial distances and time frames. Since the narrative is an illusion produced entirely through editing, and the film is aware of this and comments on it, it is an extremely useful film for thinking about the functions and procedures of film form.

Thoroughly hybrid genre

Just as it flamboyantly crosses territories and times, the film interrogates the boundaries of genre. in its DVD release, does not package itself as a documentary, but it does use, and subvert, many of the recognizable formats of documentary, principally the first-person interview mode.

Questions about location of culture

Just as it interrogates the generic boundaries between documentary and fiction, the film -- and this is characteristic of much twenty-first century film production -- inhabits unstable spatial co-ordinates, existing more in the space of media flows than in any localizable framework of empirical reference. It inserts itself very specifically in this space of flows, calling into question the ties between culture and territory.

The power of/in the filmic gaze

But it also emphasizes the uneven power relationships involved in the commodification of cultures through the mass media. Questions are raised by examining the complicity between the our gaze as privileged spectators and the global economic forces that turn Cuba into a spectacle of underdevelopment, and force Cuban girls, like the protagonist, to work in the sex-tourism trade.

Linguistic joke and the question of naming

Juliette is the European spelling of the romantic cultural icon. It's displaced here by the popular, hybridized, Cuban spelling (Yuliet). Deborah Martin, in an article on the film, suggests that the linguistic slippage here, together with many other such examples of linguistic and visual punning in the film, “discourages any spectatorial equation between visibility and truth” [“Spectatorship, Performance, Resistance: Carlos Marcovich's ¿Quién diablos es Juliette?”, Journal of Latin American Cultural Studies: Travesia 15, no. 3 (December 2006): 348].

This is vitally important, because the film sets itself up as a true-life story, yet from the outset it flags the power relationships embedded in language as well as in visual culture. It strongly suggests that the misnomer (i.e. misnaming) lies at the heart of all acts of representation.

If you take ONE thing away from this lecture, I hope it's this. It's something you must never forget in dealing with film made on or about subaltern subjects. Such film is always a misnomer, there is no transparency of representation, visibility does NOT equal truth.

It's important to note that Yuliet corrects the mis-spelling of her name, but that the misspelling persists in the title of the film, and on the DVD packaging, again emphasizing that the film does not necessarily deal with the real Yuliet, but with a fictive construction of her: Juliette.

Yuliet also makes clear here that these visual jokes are scripted: they're the director's idea, not hers, calling into question her power as a subaltern subject to resist the film's construction of her.

Always show awareness of specificity of medium

I want to say a few general things about reading film and writing about it -- and these have less to do with the location of culture and the power embedded in representation, than with the need to pay attention to what makes the medium you are writing about specific.

My advice to you as you start the course is, in your essays and in the way you read film, that you should always be thinking about what is specific to the medium.

Film

There is some crossover of course between film and photography, and questions concerning the materiality of celluloid, or the immateriality of the digital medium nowadays are very important, together with issues such as the type of lens chosen, the sensitivity of the film stock. But in terms of film form and film language, these are the major areas you should pay attention to:
Mise-en-scène

This doesn't just mean the setting. It literally means "putting onto the scene or the stage", and encompasses all of the decisions taken in staging the film. What props are chosen? Against what background is a sequence shot? Is it in a studio or on location? Has the location been arranged, etc.

The Shot

Here you need to go through the lists of various shots you'll find in some of the essays under this topic on Moodle. You want to be able to describe the various shots, as well as the sequence of shots. And note that the default word you should use when writing about film is "sequence" rather than "scene". Why?

Aerial shot, overhead shot, tracking shot, two shot, close up, extreme close up, high-angle shot, insert shot, etc., etc.

Along with the shot, consider the take. Is a sequence in fact one long take, with no editing? Or is it a rapid sequence of different shot angles? And what is the effect of a long take, or rapid editing?

The frame

This is similar to photography: you need to consider the way in which the frame creates meaning effects. By choosing to show certain things and not others, by providing off-frame sound that suggests what is outside of the visible frame, and so on.

Editing and montage

Editing is the splicing together of shots to tell a story or otherwise produce a narrative. This is crucial to ¿Quién diablos es Juliette? because the narrative in the film is entirely the product of outrageous editing. Montage has two meanings, either the superposition of two shots, so you see two images in one frame, or it is used to indicate temporal juxtaposition: a rapid cut or dissolve from one shot to another produces a superposition in the spectator's mind.

Narrative

Under narrative you need to consider issues such as temporal continuity, the use of flashback and flash-forward, and the different levels of diegesis through which the film can move. Always remember that the narrative is an effect of editing. Narratological film theorists say that the film's discourse or language -- i.e. film form -- produces the narrative, and they often use the French: discours for filmic language, and histoire for the narrative that is produced.
Remember: discours does NOT equal histoire
Sound

Sound is crucial to film after the silent era, and the shift has profound effects. But remember that sound can belong to different narrative levels: it can be intra-diegetic or it can be extra-diegetic, and sometimes music that we think is extra-diegetic, e.g. the film's score, turns out to be sound that is heard by the characters within the diegesis, i.e. , within the film. There can also be intra-diegetic and extra-diegetic narrators.

Form (feature, documentary)

Form can have a very broad meaning, and can be used to designate the filmic language, i.e. all of the above, or it can refer to different types of film such as documentary, feature, mockumentary, etc. Note that this is not the same as genre.

Genre

Genre is the term used to describe whether a film belongs to a group or family of film types, such as the thriller, Sci-Fi, film noir, etc. An important thing to consider is what happens to some of these genres when they travel: for example, is Latin American Sci-Fi the same as US Sci-Fi, or does the displacement produce a framing effect?

The star system

Finally, the star system is very important in popular cinema, such as Mexican or Brazilian film in the Golden Age of the 1940s and 1950s. But it can also be very important in art-house film, so pay attention to actors and to the way certain sign systems acrue to particular actors.

Discussion of form should never be end in itself

As I think is suggested by that clip, discussion of film form should never be an end in itself. On its own, it has about as much value as counting syllables in a poem. So, while you are expected to be able to describe the main features of a film, you must always also think what these features mean, what effect they are producing.

Form has context-dependent meaning

Form will always have a meaning or effect within a particular context of production and reception, which also means within specific cultural contexts.

Feminist approaches to film

The power vectors at work in ¿Quién diablos es Juliette? can also, of course, be read along the lines of gender, and here the film both plays on a "titillating" representation of its female subjects as objects of desire, and at the same time frames this representation self-consciously. As a (female) child, in the technical sense, questions of subaltern representation are concentrated on Yuliet.

Laura Mulvey

A classic theoretical approach to understanding the conventional representation of women in cinema is given by Laura Mulvey. I am going to cite it at the beginning of the course in order to get it out of the way hereafter:

'Woman as image, man as bearer of the look. In a world ordered by sexual imbalance, pleasure in looking has been split between active/male and passive/female. The determining male gaze projects its fantasy onto the female figure which is styled accordingly. In their traditional exhibitionist role women are simultaneously looked at and displayed, with their appearance coded for strong visual and erotic impact so that they can be said to connote to-be-looked-at-ness.'

Laura Mulvey, 'Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema' (see bibliography) p. 750.
Do not quote this passage in your essays!

This passage is extremely well known, and is perhaps the most over-quoted piece of film theory on the planet. By all means refer to the ideas, but use them to build your argument. You can assume that your supervisors and examiners know what you are referring to when you mention Mulvey's theory of the gendered nature of the gaze and of the relationship of women to spectacle. So use this as a building block towards more complex feminist theories.

The construction of Yuliet and Fabiola

The story of Yuliet is the story of her mediatization, constructed as double or twin of the Mexican Fabiola initially through the Benny Ibarra music video, and then through a film which will engineer a re-encounter with her father who had left Cuba when she was little, get her a visa, and set her up with a Mexican modelling agency. Much is made of the supposed likeness between Yuliet and Fabiola, but for most of the film they are presented as visually dissimilar, thus emphasizing the artificiality of the parallels which erase the difference between the two young women in the artificial space constructed by the mass media.

Yet the director also attempts to "give" Yuliet some feminist awareness about the situations it places her in. An extended sequence deals with her reaction to seeing Salma Hayek playing Alma in El callejón de los milagros (a famous Mexican film about a young woman seduced into semi-prostitution by false promises of a life of glamour). Salma Hayek is also interviewed. The aim seems to be to allow Yuliet to decide whether or not to insert herself within the representational systems of the Mexican fashion industry, following in the steps of Fabiola.

Needless to say, this blatant interventionism in the girl’s life breaks most of the ethical, as well as aesthetic, boundaries usually drawn within documentary film-making; or, to put it another way, the film does not disavow the interventionism which is inherent in the unequal power relationships at the heart of ethnographic-derived documentary.

Avoid naive concepts
of realism in film!

As the great Mexican filmmaker Ripstein put it, cinema is always a lie: "El cine es la mentira más maravillosa, porque cuando uno lo proyecta en la pantalla se está creyendo realmente lo que mira."

Please be VERY CAREFUL about how you use the words "real", "realism", "authentic", "authenticity", "true" and "truth" when writing about film, even documentary. Do not use these words naively. They will almost always have to be in quotation marks, and you should be critically addressing the capacity of film to represent the "real". Always remember that "realism" is an -ism, i.e. a style that produces an effect of the real.

Documentary theory

This also goes to the heart of the paradoxes of the documentary film form.

One of the most influential theorists of documentary film is Bill Nichols. These are some of the modes of documentary he has identified: the  expository, observational, interactive, reflective, performative and poetic modes.

The question of authenticity in documentary has been a spectre haunting it from the earliest newsreel re-enactment of battles for the camera onward. Most of the battle footage from the turn of the twentieth century was staged, including fake footage of key events in the Spanish-American War of 1898 (enhanced by off-camera cigar smoke) and the staging of battles by fully equipped mock armies during the Boer War of 1899-1902. As is well known, in Mexico much has been made of the inference that the exigencies of the camera influenced the course of historical events. Francisco Villa is purported to have signed a contract for US$25,000 with the Mutual Film Corporation in 1914 to film the battle of Ojinaga, in which he granted exclusive film rights, allegedly promised to fight whenever possible only during daylight hours, and delayed fighting for the arrival of the cameramen.

In the documentary form, which developed out of such reportage, the manipulation of documentary subjects to produce reality effects at the core of the form’s generic conventions. The oft-cited example is the fact that in the first feature-length documentary, Robert Flaherty’s 1922 ethnographic film about the Inuit, Nanook of the North, “a story of life and love in the actual Arctic” as the film’s tag-line put it, much of the action was staged to make it conform to preconceived notions of an “authentic” Inuit way of life. 

Recent theorization of documentary has charted the rise of the “performative mode”, which establishes a tension, Nichols suggests, “between performance and document":

Bill Nichols, Representing Reality: expository, observational, interactive, reflective, performative, poetic

"Performative documentary suspends realist representation. Performative documentary puts the referential aspect of the message in brackets, under suspension. Realism finds itself deferred, dispersed, interrupted, and postponed. [...] Performative documentary does not hide its signifieds in the guise of a referent it effortlessly pulls from a hat. These films stress their tone and expressive qualities while also retaining a referential claim to the historical. They address the challenge of giving meaning to historical events through the evocations they provide for them" (Nichols, Blurred Boundaries, 97-98).

From: Bill Nichols, Blurred Boundaries: Questions of Meaning in Contemporary Culture (Bloomington: Indiana University Press, 1994).

The location of culture

Another major point which I'd like you to be aware of throughout your course is the "location of culture".

The term is taken from Homi Bhabha's book of postcolonial theory; and along with Bhabha, in the broadest sense of his arguments, I'd like you never to forget that there is a politics and a power surrounding the location of culture

In relation to your own analyses of Ibero-American visual culture, always ask yourself these kinds of questions:

    • Who is doing the looking?
    • Who has the right to look? Who or what is excluded from the gaze?
    • What legitimizes cultural traffic, and in which directions?
    • Out of what specific historical and geographical determinants does a particular cultural product emerge?
    • Just as importantly, what are the institutional, political, ideological, historical discourses which legitimate your own location in the circuit of production and consumption of Ibero-American culture?

(Geo)politics of vision
Who has right to gaze?

In a discussion of Nancy Scheper-Hughes' study of infant mortality in the slums of a sugar- plantation town in the Brazilian Nordeste, the science studies theorist Donna Haraway asks a series of disturbing questions about visibility and invisibility:

"How is visibility possible? For whom, by whom, and of whom? What remains invisible, to whom, and why? For those peoples who are excluded from the visualizing apparatuses of the disciplinary regimes of modern power-knowledge networks, the averted gaze can be as deadly as the all-seeing panopticon that surveys the subjects of the biopolitical state."

A lot of Ibero-American visual culture is, I think, raising such questions, especially where its subjects -- photographic, cinematic, artistic -- are people who are usually excluded from the national or global gaze. Indeed, we often have to face the paradox that the very apparatus of visual culture is immersed in regimes of visibility and invisibility. How can it, therefore, hope to trace the invisible lives of those excluded from such regimes?

Conclusion: Actual/actuar

The famous early critic of the documentary film form, John Grierson, famously defined it as “the creative treatment of actuality” [John Grierson, Grierson on Documentary, Abridged ed (London: Faber, 1979), 35]. As we have seen, there has always been a slippage between documentation and fiction, between reference and fabulation, between the actorial and the actual, at the heart of documentary debate and practice. This, I think, is the meaning of this final clip from the film.

CLIPS 8 & 9

¿Quién diablos es Juliette? situates itself playfully in the gap between the illusion of immediacy, the actual or here-and-now which haunts documentary, and actuar (acting), the primacy of performance, the artificial construction of reality effects. By emphasizing this slippage, the film is able to point to all of the processes, systems and power vectors involved in the construction of filmic subjects. It also provides us with an understanding of the power of the media to connect vast spatial and cultural differences in ways that dissolve the local even as they construct new forms of culture and identity.
